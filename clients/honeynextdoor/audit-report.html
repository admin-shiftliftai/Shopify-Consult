The bottom line

Your brand voice — "unheated, unfiltered, and generally not messed with" — is better than most agencies write, and honey sold by the buyer's own Atlanta neighborhood is one of the strongest small-brand ideas we've ever audited. But that idea is hidden inside a dropdown, roughly a quarter of your catalog is sold-out dead weight teaching first-time visitors the store is inactive, and one product carries a medical claim plus a leftover tracking pixel from an old marketplace. The three fixes below aren't conversion nitpicks — they're brand-defining, and the first one is the foundation for gifting, SEO, and wholesale all at once.

Your top 3 fixes

1

Fix the elderberry listing today — it's a legal & technical liability

Compliance · Critical
What we found
The Elderberry Syrup description makes explicit medical claims — "Clinically shown to help boost your immune system… will lessen the severity and length of your illness" — and contains a leftover eBay tracking pixel (<img src="//rover.ebay.com/…">) pasted in from an old listing, firing a third-party tracker on your store.
Why it costs you
Disease-treatment claims on a food product are the kind of wording the FDA/FTC send warning letters over — a real risk, not a conversion tweak. The pixel leaks visitor data to a marketplace you don't sell on and can trip browser security warnings.
Exactly what to do
Products → Elderberry Syrup → edit HTML: delete the <img> tag and replace the medical sentences with compliant wellness framing plus the standard "not evaluated by the FDA… not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease" disclaimer. Paste-ready copy is included with this report.
Impact: risk removal — do this firstEffort: 15 min, DIY
2

Your best idea in the world is hidden in a dropdown

Highest upside
What we found
Neighborhood honey — Buckhead, Inman Park, Grant Park, 12 in all — exists only as variants of generic products, and the variant option is mislabeled 1lb where it should read "Neighborhood." Variants get no Google pages, no individual photos, and no shareable link to "Buckhead honey."
Why it costs you
"A Neighborhood Honey Company" is literally your tagline, yet a shopper can't see the neighborhoods, search engines can't index them, and a gift-buyer can't send a friend their own street's jar. Your signature asset is invisible.
Exactly what to do
Today: rename the variant option to "Neighborhood" (10 min). This month: build a "Shop by Neighborhood" collection + homepage block with a labeled jar photo each. Next: split into per-neighborhood pages so each ranks ("Buckhead Raw Honey") and unlocks realtor closing-gift and corporate channels.
Impact: compounds into SEO + gifting + wholesaleEffort: minutes → a weekend, phased
3

A quarter of the catalog is dead weight

Priority
What we found
Five of 20 products are fully sold out yet still in the main grid: tumblers (dead since 2020), Truffle, Gallberry, all Sourwood (copy still brags about the "finest ever" 2019 harvest), and 7 of 8 soap scents.
Why it costs you
Every dead click is a bounce, and a grid full of "Sold out" teaches a first-time visitor the store is inactive — poison for trust before the first purchase.
Exactly what to do
Unpublish the tumblers (or make them a waitlist). For seasonal honeys, swap the dead "Sold Out" card for a "back in season — get notified" email capture and sort them to the bottom. Refresh the stale vintage copy.
Impact: typ. +1–3% CR + an owned email list from your most-wanted productsEffort: ~1 hr, DIY

Full findings

Catalog & product data

  • Generic titles — "Soap", "Candles", "Organic Lip Balm" — with no benefit or keywords (compare your excellent Sweet Clover copy).
  • Most products carry a single photo; the Tunisian candle has 32 variants (4 colors × 8 scents) and one image — buyers can't see what they're choosing.
  • No reviews or star ratings visible anywhere — your repeat-customer proof is missing at the point of decision.
  • Gallon jars say "PICKUP ONLY" in the copy but are set requires_shipping: true — checkout will sell a $124–$170 gallon to someone across the country.
  • Copy inconsistency: "130 hives / twelve apiaries" vs "150 hives / fifteen apiaries." Product types are mis-tagged (beeswax & candles typed "Honey"), which breaks automated collections and filters.
  • "Scratch and Dent Honey" is a great offer hiding under a clearance name — reframe (e.g. "Ugly Jar Club") and it becomes a content asset.

Offer, pricing & first impression

  • Zero bundles or gift sets in a 20-product catalog built for gifting — the single biggest untapped AOV lever (honey + candle + lip balm = "Taste of [Neighborhood]" box).
  • Blurry, out-of-focus hero on a carousel while your product shots are crisp — use one sharp image, keep the single SHOP NOW CTA, drop the slider.
  • No announcement bar working for you (shipping threshold, farmers-market schedule, or seasonal drop).
  • "Hive Removal" (a service) sits mid-nav in a retail store — move it under About/FAQ; add "Shop by Neighborhood" or "Best Sellers" instead.

What you're already doing right

  • Brand voice is a genuine moat — "generally not messed with," the cease-and-desist tumbler joke, the whole tone.
  • Neighborhood concept + hive-removal-to-honey story = authenticity most brands only fake.
  • "Honey on the Side" farm-partnership program is smart line expansion with honest provenance.
  • Price laddering within products (3oz → 1lb → 2lb → gallon) is already in place.

What we'd verify next

Four scoring areas weren't accessible in this pass and could each hold meaningful wins: the mobile product page (sticky add-to-cart, tap targets), cart & checkout (express pay, guest checkout, shipping surprises), email capture & abandoned-cart flow, and your homepage title tag (Google currently indexes it as "…Local Atlanta Bees - Tupelo Honey," which looks misconfigured). One price to reconcile: the product feed lists Bourbon Barrel at $12 while the storefront grid shows $17.

This is the natural scope of an ongoing optimization engagement.
